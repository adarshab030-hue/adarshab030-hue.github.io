What is the Exotic Fracture Object (EFO) Theory?

The Exotic Fracture Object (EFO) Theory is a scientific explanation proposed to understand the unusual interstellar object ʻOumuamua (1I/2017), which passed through our Solar System in 2017.

Unlike typical comets or asteroids, ʻOumuamua showed several strange behaviors: it had an extremely elongated shape, showed no visible comet tail, rotated chaotically, and experienced a small but measurable acceleration without any detected gas release.

The EFO Theory suggests that ʻOumuamua is a natural fragment formed during violent stellar events such as supernova shock waves, tidal disruption near stars, or high-energy collisions between planetary bodies. These extreme events can break solid material into thin, strong, and volatile-poor fragments unlike objects normally found in our Solar System.

According to this idea, ʻOumuamua’s motion can be explained by subtle surface-level forces — such as thermal desorption or radiation-driven effects — rather than traditional cometary outgassing. This allows the object to accelerate without producing a visible coma.

The EFO framework introduces a new category of interstellar objects, expanding our understanding of how planetary systems evolve and how extreme astrophysical processes shape solid matter across the galaxy.
